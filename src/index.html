Let's take a closer look at 1,987 aircraft incidents from January 1, 1995 to June 26, 2016


The manufacturers of the aircrafts involved in these incidents were any of the following: Bombardier, Boeing, McDonnell-Douglas, Embraer, or Airbus.

When there is an aircraft incident, the number of people injured by said incident is reported. This metric is made up of both serious and fatal injuries.

The total number of injuries over time, as seen to the left, does not appear to have any easily-discernable of trend line. Although it does appear to taper off from 2010 on, with 2014 being an exception. There are particularly high years, such as 1996 and particularly low years such as 2011

Let's look closer at Bombardier


If we filter the data to examine only incidents involving Bombardier manufactured aircraft, we get what we see to the left. As we can see, a small fraction of all injuries from these incidents involved Bombardier aircraft.

Bombardier is a Canadian transportation manufacturer. Bombardier has historically been a small player in commercial airliners. Much of the aircraft they manufacture are private jets. In fact, they have recently removed themselves from commercial airline manufacturing altogether.

The underrepresentation of their aircraft in commercial airline fleets is likely why their number of injuries are so low. Incidents involving private jets will injure fewer people than those involving commercial ones.

Now let's take a closer look at Embraer

Embraer is another smaller player in aircraft manufacturing. They are a Brazilian company which manufactures commercial, military, private, and agricultural aircraft. The commercial aircraft they manufacture are largely small regional jets which often hold from 70 to 124 passengers.

As one of the smaller manufacturers, both in representation in the industry and in size of products, Embraer would be expected to have some of the lowest injury counts by year of the five manufacturers explored here. And this proves to be the case, as seen to the left.

Embraer does appear to be poised to grow their position in the commercial aircraft industry, however. As was recently announced in 2019, a joint venture between Embraer and Boeing is under works.

Let's take a closer look at McDonnell Douglas

McDonnell Douglas is an American manufactuer of commercial and military aircraft. It was purchased by Boeing in 1997, which is now it's parent company.

In the realm of commercial aircraft, McDonnell Douglas tends to make mid-size aircraft that are used for regional service. As a result, they tend to carry fewer passengers than other larger players like Airbus and Boeing. For example, one of their most popular jets is the MD-83, which has a maximum passenger capacity of 155.

So their total injuries should be expected to be lower overall. There are a few years where they rival Airbus, however.

Let's take a closer look at Airbus

Airbus is a European aircraft manufacturer. As one of the largest commercial aircraft manufacturer in the world, Airbus manufactured jets carry hundreds of thousands of parssengers every day.The most popular Airbus jet is the A380, which has a capacity of 853 passengers.

Likely because of the volume of passengers they ferry every day, they have much higher reported injuries than smaller aircraft manufacturing players like Embraer and Bombardier.

As seen to the left, their peak year for injuries was 2009. Again, There doesn't look to be a discernable trend. Airbus has very low years and very high years. This suggests that crashes are uncommon, and a single one will raise their numbers significantly for that year. Particularly since the capacity of their jets is so high.

But what about Boeing?

Boeing, an American aircraft manufacturer, is currently the largest manufacturer of commercially-used aircraft. With over 40% of the commercial aircraft in use today being of strong>Boeing-make. As mentioned earlier, they continue to grow their reach with mergers and strategic partnerships worldwide.

As such a large manufactuer, you would expect Boeing to have among the highest rates of injury out of the five manufacturers explored. When your aircraft make up over 40% of the market, they'll also be involved in many of the crashes. Adding to this likelihood is the size of their aircraft. Their one of their most popular jets, for example, the Boeing 777 has a passenger capacity of 550.

Boeing has had a bad time of it recently, with the disasters involving the Boeing 737 MAX. In general, however, excluding a few outlier years, their injuries have been trending downward since their peak in 1996. 1996 was the year of the TWA Flight 800 crash, the third-deadliest aviation incident in American history and the Faucett Flight 251 crash, the deadliest aircraft accident in Peruvian history.
